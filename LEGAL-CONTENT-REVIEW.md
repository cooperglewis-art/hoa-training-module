## MODULE 1: Understanding Governing Documents

### Slide 1: Your Governing Documents & the Hierarchy

**Teaching Content:**

The course teaches that Texas community associations operate under a strict hierarchy:

| Level | Document | What It Controls | How It's Changed |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 (Top) | Federal & Texas Law | Constitutional rights, Property Code, FCC rules | Legislative process only |
| 2 | Texas Property Code (Ch. 202, 209, 82) | Mandatory procedures for HOAs/POAs/COAs | Legislative amendment |
| 3 | Declaration (CC&Rs) | Property use restrictions, assessments, enforcement powers | Membership vote (typically 67%) |
| 4 | Articles of Incorporation | Corporate existence and purpose | Board and/or membership vote |
| 5 | Bylaws | Internal governance: elections, meetings, board structure | Usually membership vote |
| 6 (Bottom) | Rules & Regulations | Day-to-day operations: pool hours, parking | Board vote |

**Key claims to verify:**
- [ ] CC&Rs are recorded in county deed records and "run with the land"
- [ ] CC&Rs bind every current and future owner
- [ ] Bylaws do not create property use restrictions
- [ ] Rules & Regulations must be consistent with CC&Rs
- [ ] A board rule that contradicts the Declaration is unenforceable
- [ ] Texas courts interpret ambiguous CC&R provisions in favor of the homeowner's free use of property
- [ ] The hierarchy ordering shown above is accurate for Texas

**Scenario: The 30-Pound Dog Rule**
- CC&Rs say homeowners may keep "common household pets"
- Board passes rule banning dogs over 30 pounds
- Homeowner with 50-pound Labrador challenges
- **Claim:** Board's rule contradicts CC&Rs and is unenforceable. Board could regulate pet behavior (leashes, waste) but can't use a board rule to rewrite the CC&Rs without a formal amendment vote.
- [ ] Is this analysis correct under Texas law?

### Slide 2: Texas Property Code & Your Association

**Key statutory claims to verify:**

| Statute | Claim Made | Verify |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| § 209.007 — Notice & Hearing | Before levying a fine or filing suit: (1) Send written notice by certified or verified mail, (2) Give owner a reasonable time to cure (for curable violations), (3) Owner has 30 days to request a hearing where they can present their side. Skip any step = entire enforcement action is invalid. | [X] CORRECTED: The 30-day period is for requesting a hearing, not a fixed cure period. The statute requires a "reasonable time" to cure curable violations. Also, "verified mail" may satisfy the delivery requirement depending on current statutory language. |
| § 209.005 — Records Access | Must provide books/records within 10 business days of written request. Penalty: $500/day, up to $5,000, recoverable by owner in court. | [X] CORRECTED: Section number is 209.005; penalty cap is $5,000, not $10,000. |
| § 209.009 — Foreclosure Limits | Cannot foreclose for fines alone — only unpaid assessments. | [ ] Accurate? |
| § 209.00593 — ADR Requirement | Written offer of mediation/ADR before filing suit. Owner gets 30 days to accept. Skip = lawsuit may be dismissed. | [ ] Accurate? |
| § 202.012 — Flag Display | Cannot ban U.S., Texas, or military flags. HOA may regulate size/placement but cannot prohibit display. Restriction is void regardless of CC&Rs. | [X] CORRECTED: Section is 202.012, not 202.010. The 4'x6' size limit applies to political signs under Election Code § 259.002, not flags. Verify specific flag size provisions with counsel. |

- [ ] Chapter 82 is less prescriptive on enforcement procedures than Chapter 209
- [ ] State law overrides CC&Rs where they conflict
- [ ] CC&Rs can be stricter than the statute but never less protective of homeowners

## MODULE 2: The Enforcement Process

### Slide 1: Identifying Violations & Proper Documentation

**Key claims to verify:**
- [ ] Texas courts construe restrictive covenants strictly
- [ ] If a CC&R provision is vague or ambiguous, a court will interpret it in favor of the homeowner's free use of property
- [ ] "'Maintain your yard in good condition' is harder to enforce than 'Lawn height shall not exceed 8 inches'" — is this an accurate characterization of how Texas courts treat specificity?
- [ ] Selective enforcement defense: if an association enforces against one homeowner but ignores the same violation by others, future violators can raise this defense

- **Claim:** Court may find the board waived its right to enforce, or must begin enforcement against all violators simultaneously.

### Slide 2: The Notice, Hearing & Fine Process

**Chapter 209 Enforcement Process taught:**

1. Send First Written Notice (Certified or Verified Mail) — describe violation, cite CC&R provision, give a reasonable time to cure
2. Allow a Reasonable Cure Period — the owner also has 30 days to request a hearing to contest
3. Send Notice of Hearing (If Not Cured / If Hearing Requested) — certified or verified mail, hearing date/time/location, owner's right to attend
4. Conduct the Hearing — present evidence, let homeowner respond, take minutes. If homeowner doesn't attend, may proceed.
5. Issue Written Decision — findings, fine amount, CC&R provision violated. Send by certified or verified mail.

**Claims to verify:**
- [ ] Is this 5-step process an accurate summary of § 209.007 requirements?
- [ ] Certified or verified mail is required (not regular mail, email, or door notice)?
- [ ] A "reasonable time" to cure is the statutory standard (not a fixed 30-day cure period)?
- [ ] The 30-day period is for the owner to request a hearing?
- [ ] The board may proceed with a hearing if the homeowner was properly notified but chose not to attend?
- [ ] Fine authority must come from the CC&Rs or bylaws — board cannot create it independently?
- [ ] § 209.009: Cannot foreclose on a property for unpaid fines alone?
- [ ] If a homeowner owes fines only (no unpaid assessments), the only collection option is a civil lawsuit?

**Scenario: The Notice That Got Everything Wrong**
- Notice says: "Your property is not in compliance with community standards. Please correct the situation within 10 days or fines will be assessed."
- **Claim:** This fails because: (1) doesn't identify specific violation, (2) doesn't cite CC&R provision, (3) gives only 10 days — unlikely to be a "reasonable time" to cure, (4) threatens fines without mentioning the owner's right to request a hearing, (5) sent by regular mail instead of certified or verified mail.
- [ ] Are all five identified deficiencies correct under § 209.007?

## MODULE 3: Pre-Lawsuit, Counsel & Alternatives

### Slide 1: Evidence, Escalation & Knowing When to Act

**Claims to verify:**
- [ ] Once litigation is reasonably anticipated, there is a legal duty to preserve all relevant evidence (litigation hold)
- [ ] Deleting records after a dispute arises can constitute spoliation of evidence
- [ ] A court may instruct the jury to assume destroyed evidence was unfavorable to the association
- [ ] Paying a fine does not resolve an ongoing violation — fines are a collection remedy, not a compliance remedy
- [ ] Injunctive relief (court order to stop the conduct) requires legal counsel and likely litigation

**Scenario: The Daycare Dilemma**
- CC&Rs prohibit "commercial use"
- Homeowner runs in-home daycare
- Has county home occupation permit
- **Claim:** Both sides have a reasonable argument about whether "commercial use" covers a small daycare. The government permit complicates the association's position. This is when to bring in legal counsel.
- [ ] Is "commercial use" genuinely ambiguous when applied to in-home daycares under Texas law?
- [ ] Does a county home occupation permit affect enforceability of CC&R restrictions?

### Slide 2: ADR, Mediation & Working with Counsel

**Claims to verify:**
- [ ] § 209.00593: Before filing suit (except for unpaid assessments), association must make written offer of ADR. Homeowner has 30 days to accept. Skip = lawsuit may be dismissed.
- [ ] Mediation is non-binding unless both parties agree to a settlement
- [ ] Arbitration is binding with limited appeal rights
- [ ] Mediation cost: $500-$3,000 typical
- [ ] Arbitration cost: $3,000-$15,000 typical
- [ ] Litigation cost: $10,000-$100,000+ typical
- [ ] Association's attorney represents the association as an entity, not individual board members
- [ ] If a board member discloses attorney's legal advice to a third party, attorney-client privilege may be waived
- [ ] Legal strategy discussions should only occur in closed executive session

**Scenario: The $2,000 Solution vs. the $50,000 Lawsuit**
- Homeowner built patio without approval, $4,500 in fines, claims selective enforcement
- Mediation produced creative deal: after-the-fact architectural review, modify patio, reduced $1,500 fine
- **Claim:** Total mediation cost $2,000. Litigation alternative: $25,000-$50,000 with uncertain outcome.
- [ ] Are these cost estimates realistic for Texas HOA disputes?

## FINAL ASSESSMENT — Case Study

**Scenario:**
"You are advising the board of Green Valley HOA. A neighbor has complained that the homeowner at 123 Oak Street has been operating a commercial auto repair business out of their garage for the past 6 months. Customers come and go daily, and there is signage visible from the street. Section 4.2 of the CC&Rs states: 'No lot shall be used, in whole or in part, for any commercial, business, or professional purpose.' The board has never addressed a commercial use violation before and wants to know how to proceed."

### Question 1:
"What should the board's FIRST step be upon receiving this complaint?"

**Correct answer:** "Verify the CC&R provision, document the violation, and send a formal written notice via certified or verified mail citing Section 4.2 with a reasonable time to cure"

**Explanation:** "Before taking any enforcement action, the board must verify that a specific, enforceable CC&R provision has been violated. Here, Section 4.2 clearly prohibits commercial use. The board should then document the violation with date-stamped photographs from public areas and send a formal violation notice via certified or verified mail citing Section 4.2, with a reasonable time to cure — as required by Chapter 209. The owner also has 30 days to request a hearing."

- [ ] Correct sequence? Should documentation come before or after the notice?

### Question 2:
"The board sends proper notice. After a reasonable cure period, the homeowner has not cured the violation. What must happen BEFORE the board can levy a fine?"

**Correct answer:** "The board must provide notice of a hearing where the homeowner can attend and present their side before any fine is levied"

**Explanation:** "Under § 209.007, after the cure period expires without compliance, the association must send a notice informing the homeowner of a hearing before the board or a board-appointed committee."

- [ ] Correct? Is a certified or verified mail notice specifically required for the hearing?

### Question 3:
"At the hearing, the homeowner argues that Section 4.2 does not apply because it says 'commercial, business, or professional purpose' and auto repair is a 'trade,' not a 'business.' How should the board evaluate this defense?"

**Correct answer:** "Recognize that the broad language ('commercial, business, or professional purpose') clearly covers operating an auto repair shop with customers and signage, regardless of how the homeowner labels the activity"

**Explanation:** "Section 4.2 broadly prohibits commercial, business, and professional use. Operating an auto repair shop with customers, signage, and daily traffic clearly falls within a general prohibition on commercial activity — regardless of how the homeowner labels it."

- [ ] Correct? Would a Texas court agree that "commercial, business, or professional purpose" is broad enough to cover auto repair?
- [ ] Is the claim that "Texas courts look at the substance of the activity, not the label" accurate?

### Question 4:
"After a proper hearing, the board levies a fine. The homeowner pays the fine but continues operating the auto repair business. The board wants to stop the activity permanently. What is the correct next step?"

**Correct answer:** "Make a written offer of alternative dispute resolution as required by § 209.00593, and if declined, consult counsel about seeking injunctive relief in court"

**Explanation:** "Payment of fines does not cure an ongoing violation — fines are a collection remedy, not a compliance remedy. Under § 209.00593, before filing suit, the association must first make a written offer of ADR and give the homeowner 30 days to accept. Foreclosure is not available because § 209.009 prohibits foreclosure for fines alone."

- [ ] Correct sequence: ADR offer first, then litigation?
- [ ] Confirm § 209.009 prohibition on foreclosure for fines alone applies here
